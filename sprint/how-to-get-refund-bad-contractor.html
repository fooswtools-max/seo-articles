How to Get a Refund from a Bad Contractor — Complete 2026 Guide

Step-by-step guide to getting your money back from a bad contractor. Covers demand letters, small claims court, state contractor boards, and chargeback options.

When Contractor Work Goes Wrong

Hiring a contractor who does bad work, abandons your project, or refuses to fix problems is one of the most frustrating financial situations homeowners face. The good news: you have multiple legal options to recover your money, and most of them do not require hiring an expensive lawyer.

This guide covers every option available to you in 2026, from informal resolution to small claims court and state board complaints.

Step 1: Document Everything (Before Taking Action)

Before contacting the contractor or anyone else, document the current state of everything:

  • Take detailed photos and videos of all deficient work from multiple angles
  • Save every text message, email, and voicemail from the contractor
  • Gather the original contract, change orders, and all payment receipts
  • Get 2-3 written estimates from other licensed contractors to fix or complete the work
  • Check if any required permits were pulled and if inspections were done

Step 2: Send a Formal Demand Letter

A demand letter is your first official step. It puts the contractor on notice and creates a paper trail for court. Your letter should include:

  1. A clear statement of what was promised vs. what was delivered
  2. The exact dollar amount you are demanding as a refund
  3. A deadline of 14-30 days to respond
  4. A statement that you will file in small claims court if they do not comply

Send it via certified mail with return receipt. Keep a copy of everything.

Step 3: File a Complaint with the State Contractor Board

Every state has a licensing board or regulatory agency for contractors. Filing a complaint is free and can result in:

  • Investigation of the contractor's work
  • Fines against the contractor
  • License suspension or revocation
  • Restitution orders requiring the contractor to pay you back

Search for "[your state] contractor licensing board complaint" to find the filing process. Most accept online complaints.

Step 4: Small Claims Court (If They Do Not Pay)

If the demand letter and board complaint do not resolve the issue, file in small claims court. The process is straightforward:

  1. Determine your state's small claims limit and filing location
  2. File the claim (typically $30-$75 filing fee)
  3. Serve the contractor with court papers
  4. Present your evidence at the hearing
  5. Collect your judgment

Judges see contractor disputes regularly and are sympathetic to homeowners who documented their case well.

Step 5: Credit Card Chargeback (Additional Option)

If you paid the contractor with a credit card, you may be able to file a chargeback. Contact your credit card company and explain that services were not rendered as agreed. Provide your documentation. Chargebacks can be filed up to 120 days after the transaction in most cases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a full refund from a bad contractor?

Yes, you may be entitled to a full refund if the contractor failed to complete the work, did substandard work, or abandoned the project. You can recover payments through small claims court, your state contractor board, or credit card chargebacks.

Should I hire a lawyer to sue a contractor?

For amounts under your state's small claims limit (typically $5,000-$10,000), you do not need a lawyer. Small claims court is designed for self-representation. For larger amounts, a construction attorney may be worth the investment.

How long do I have to file a claim against a contractor?

Statutes of limitation vary by state, typically 2-6 years for breach of contract and 3-10 years for construction defects. Start the process as soon as you realize the work is deficient.

What evidence do I need against a bad contractor?

Gather the original contract, all payment receipts, photos of incomplete or defective work, text messages and emails, any permits or inspection reports, and quotes from other contractors to fix the work.

Can I file a complaint with the contractor's licensing board?

Yes, and you should. Most states have a contractor licensing board that investigates complaints. This can result in the contractor losing their license, being fined, or being ordered to pay restitution.
