PSLF Application Step by Step: Don't Get Denied

Why 98% of Early PSLF Applications Were Denied

When PSLF first started processing applications in 2017, the denial rate was over 98 percent. Most applicants had the wrong loan type, wrong repayment plan, or failed to certify their employment properly. The program has improved since then, but these pitfalls still catch people. This guide walks you through every step to make sure you get approved.

Step 1: Verify Your Loans Are Direct Loans

Log into studentaid.gov and check your loan types. Only Direct Loans qualify for PSLF. If you have FFEL or Perkins loans, you must consolidate them into a Direct Consolidation Loan first. Warning: consolidation resets your payment count, so do this early.

Qualifying loan types: Direct Subsidized, Direct Unsubsidized, Direct PLUS, Direct Consolidation.

Step 2: Enroll in an Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) Plan

You must be on an income-driven repayment plan for payments to count. The best options are:

  • SAVE Plan — newest and typically the lowest payment (5-10% of discretionary income)
  • PAYE — 10% of discretionary income, capped at standard payment amount
  • IBR — 10-15% of discretionary income depending on when you borrowed
  • ICR — 20% of discretionary income (only option for Parent PLUS after consolidation)

Apply at studentaid.gov/idr. Processing takes 2 to 4 weeks. You must recertify income annually.

Step 3: Confirm Your Employer Qualifies

Qualifying employers include:

  • Government organizations at any level (federal, state, local, tribal)
  • 501(c)(3) nonprofits
  • Other nonprofits providing qualifying public services
  • AmeriCorps and Peace Corps

Use the PSLF Help Tool at studentaid.gov to verify your employer. If your employer is not in the database, you can still submit — the tool does not have every qualifying employer listed.

Step 4: Submit the Employment Certification Form (ECF) Annually

This is the step most people skip, and it causes the most problems. Submit the PSLF Form (Employment Certification) every year and every time you change employers. Your employer signs Section 4 confirming your employment dates and full-time status.

Submit electronically through MOHELA or mail the form. Keep copies of every submission. Annual certification lets you catch problems early instead of discovering them after 10 years.

Step 5: Make 120 Qualifying Payments

Each qualifying payment must be:

  • Made after October 1, 2007
  • Made under a qualifying repayment plan (IDR)
  • Made while working full-time for a qualifying employer
  • Made on time (within 15 days of the due date)
  • For the full amount due

Payments do not need to be consecutive. Track your count at MOHELA or studentaid.gov.

Step 6: Submit Your Final PSLF Application

After 120 qualifying payments, submit the PSLF application through MOHELA. Processing takes 60 to 120 days. The forgiven amount is completely tax-free at the federal level.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is PSLF?

Public Service Loan Forgiveness forgives remaining Direct Loan balance after 120 qualifying payments while working full-time for a qualifying public service employer. Tax-free.

Why do most applications get denied?

Wrong loan type (must be Direct Loans), wrong repayment plan (must be IDR), and missing Employment Certification Forms are the top reasons.

How long does PSLF take?

10 years of qualifying payments (120 months). Final application processing takes 60 to 120 days after submission.

Do payments need to be consecutive?

No. You can switch employers, take breaks, and resume counting when you return to qualifying employment.

Is PSLF forgiveness taxable?

No. PSLF forgiveness is completely tax-free at the federal level.
