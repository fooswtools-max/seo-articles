How to Collect Money After Winning Small Claims Court

Won your small claims case but the defendant won't pay? Learn exactly how to collect your judgment using wage garnishment, bank levies, and property liens.

Winning Is Only Half the Battle

Congratulations on winning your small claims case. But here is something courts do not always explain: the court does not collect the money for you. You have a judgment — a legal right to be paid — but now you need to enforce it.

Roughly 80% of small claims winners successfully collect their judgments. This guide shows you exactly how to be in that 80%.

Step 1: Wait for the Appeal Period

After the judgment, the defendant usually has 30 days to appeal. During this time, you cannot take collection action. If no appeal is filed, you can begin enforcement.

Step 2: Send a Payment Demand

Send a formal letter to the defendant with a copy of the judgment, demanding payment within 15 days. Include payment instructions (check, money order, bank transfer). Many defendants pay at this stage to avoid further legal action.

Step 3: Use Legal Collection Tools

Wage Garnishment

If the debtor has a job, wage garnishment is the most reliable collection method. File a Writ of Execution with the court, then submit an Earnings Withholding Order to the debtor's employer. The employer must withhold up to 25% of the debtor's disposable income per paycheck until the judgment is paid.

Bank Account Levy

If you know where the debtor banks, you can levy their account. File a Writ of Execution and give it to the sheriff or marshal, who will serve it on the bank. The bank freezes the account and turns over funds up to the judgment amount. This can be effective because it captures the money immediately.

Property Lien

Record your judgment with the county recorder's office where the debtor owns property. This creates a lien that prevents them from selling or refinancing without paying you first. It is a long-term strategy but very effective for debtors who own real estate.

Pro Tip: If you do not know where the debtor works or banks, file a Debtor Examination with the court. This forces the debtor to appear and disclose their income, bank accounts, and assets under oath.

Step 4: Debtor's Examination

A debtor's examination is a powerful tool. You ask the court to order the debtor to appear and answer questions about their finances under oath. You can ask about:

  • Current employer and income
  • Bank accounts and balances
  • Real estate and vehicle ownership
  • Other assets and investments
  • Other debts and obligations

If the debtor fails to appear, the court can issue a bench warrant for their arrest.

Adding Interest and Costs

Your judgment accrues interest from the date it is entered, typically at 5-10% per year depending on the state. You can also add your enforcement costs (filing fees, sheriff fees) to the total owed. Keep a running tally of all amounts.

When to Use a Collection Agency

If self-enforcement is not working, judgment collection agencies specialize in this. They use skip tracing, credit reporting, and persistent contact to collect. They typically charge 25-50% of what they recover, but 50% of something is better than 100% of nothing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if someone refuses to pay after losing in small claims court?

You have several legal tools to force collection including wage garnishment, bank account levies, property liens, and debtor examinations. The court judgment gives you legal authority to use these enforcement methods.

How long do I have to collect a small claims judgment?

Judgments are valid for 5 to 20 years depending on the state, and most can be renewed. You do not have to collect immediately — you can wait until the debtor's financial situation improves.

Can I garnish someone's wages after small claims court?

Yes, in most states. You file a wage garnishment order with the court, and the debtor's employer is required by law to withhold a portion of each paycheck and send it to you until the judgment is satisfied.

How much does it cost to enforce a judgment?

Collection costs vary: wage garnishment applications cost $10-50, bank levies cost $25-100 through the sheriff, and debtor examinations are typically free to file. Most of these costs can be added to the judgment amount.

Can I hire a collection agency for a small claims judgment?

Yes. Collection agencies that specialize in court judgments typically charge 25-50% of the amount collected. This is a good option if you do not want to handle enforcement yourself or the debtor is difficult to locate.
