Landlord Didn't Return Security Deposit? Here's What to Do

Your landlord won't return your security deposit? Step-by-step guide to getting your money back, including demand letters, small claims court, and state deadlines.

Your Landlord Owes You That Money

If your landlord has not returned your security deposit or made unfair deductions, you have strong legal rights. Every state has laws that protect tenants and set strict deadlines for landlords to return deposits. When landlords violate these laws, tenants can often recover double or triple the deposit amount as a penalty.

This guide walks you through exactly what to do — from documenting the situation to filing in small claims court if needed.

Step 1: Know Your State's Deadline

The first thing to check is whether your landlord has missed the legal deadline. Common state deadlines include:

  • California: 21 days
  • New York: 14 days
  • Texas: 30 days
  • Florida: 15 days (no dispute) or 30 days (with dispute)
  • Illinois: 30-45 days depending on the city
  • Washington: 21 days

If your landlord has exceeded this deadline, they may owe you the full deposit regardless of any actual damages, plus penalties in many states.

Step 2: Document Everything

Gather all evidence you have:

  • Your lease agreement showing the deposit amount
  • Move-in inspection report or photos
  • Move-out photos and video walkthrough
  • Any communication with the landlord about the deposit
  • Receipts for any cleaning or repairs you did before moving out
  • The itemized deduction list if the landlord provided one

Step 3: Send a Demand Letter

Before going to court, send your landlord a formal demand letter via certified mail. This letter should state the deposit amount, the date you moved out, the legal deadline they missed, and your demand for the full refund within 7-14 days.

Many disputes are resolved at this stage because landlords realize you know your rights and are prepared to take legal action.

Important: In states with penalty provisions, mention the specific statute in your demand letter. For example, in California, cite Civil Code Section 1950.5 which allows up to 2x the deposit in bad faith cases.

Step 4: File in Small Claims Court

If the demand letter does not work, file a small claims court case. Security deposit cases are among the most common and most successful in small claims court. You will need:

  1. The completed small claims court filing form for your county
  2. A copy of your demand letter and proof it was sent
  3. Your lease agreement
  4. All photos and documentation
  5. The filing fee (typically $30-75)

Step 5: Present Your Case

At the hearing, bring organized copies of all evidence. Present the facts clearly: the deposit amount, when you moved out, what the landlord deducted or failed to return, and why the deductions are unfair or the deadline was missed. Judges hear these cases regularly and tend to favor tenants who come prepared with documentation.

Common Landlord Excuses (and Why They Fail)

  • "The apartment was dirty." Normal cleaning is not damage. If you left it in reasonable condition, this deduction is invalid.
  • "The carpet needs replacing." If the carpet was already old, normal wear and tear applies. Landlords must prorate based on carpet age.
  • "I needed to repaint." Paint fading and minor scuffs are normal wear. Landlords cannot charge for repainting unless there is actual damage like large holes or stains.
  • "You broke the lease." Breaking a lease does not forfeit your deposit. The landlord can only deduct for actual damages and unpaid rent.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a landlord have to return a security deposit?

It varies by state, but most states require landlords to return the deposit within 14 to 30 days after move-out. California gives 21 days, New York 14 days, Texas 30 days, and Florida 15-30 days depending on whether deductions are disputed.

Can a landlord keep my deposit for normal wear and tear?

No. Landlords cannot deduct for normal wear and tear, which includes minor scuffs on walls, small nail holes, worn carpet from regular use, and faded paint. They can only deduct for damage beyond normal use.

What if my landlord made up fake damages?

Document everything with photos and videos during your move-out walkthrough. If the landlord fabricates damage, you can dispute the deductions and sue in small claims court. Judges frequently rule against landlords who cannot prove damages with evidence.

Can I get more than my deposit back?

Many states allow tenants to recover two to three times the deposit amount as a penalty when landlords act in bad faith. Check your state laws for specific penalty provisions.

Do I need a lawyer to sue my landlord?

No. Security deposit disputes are ideal for small claims court, which is designed for self-representation. Most cases involve straightforward facts and evidence like photos, receipts, and move-in/move-out checklists.
