Do Not Let Time Run Out on Your Case

Every legal claim has a deadline for filing, called the statute of limitations. Miss this deadline, and you lose the right to sue — no matter how strong your case is. Knowing your state's time limits is the first thing you should check before pursuing any small claims case.

Types of Claims and Their Deadlines

Written Contracts (4-10 years)

Includes lease agreements, service contracts, loan agreements with written terms, and any signed document. Written contracts generally have the longest statutes because the terms are clearly documented.

Oral Contracts (2-6 years)

Verbal agreements are legally binding but have shorter filing deadlines. This covers handshake deals, verbal promises to pay, and informal agreements without written documentation.

Property Damage (2-6 years)

Covers damage to your car, home, personal belongings, or other property. The clock starts when the damage occurred or when you discovered it.

Fraud (3-6 years)

Fraud cases often use a "discovery rule" where the clock starts when you discovered or should have discovered the fraud, not when it actually occurred.

Important: If your deadline is approaching, file immediately. You can always gather additional evidence after filing. Missing the statute of limitations means losing your case permanently.

What Pauses the Clock

Certain events can pause or "toll" the statute of limitations:

  • Defendant leaves the state: The clock pauses while they are gone in many states
  • You were a minor: The clock may not start until you turn 18
  • Defendant concealed the wrongdoing: The clock may start when you discover the issue
  • Defendant acknowledges the debt: A partial payment or written acknowledgment may restart the clock

Act Now, Not Later

Even if you have years left on your statute of limitations, filing sooner is better. Witnesses forget details, evidence gets lost, and the other party may move or become harder to find. The best time to file your case is as soon as you have your evidence together.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the statute of limitations for small claims court?

The statute of limitations is the legal deadline for filing a lawsuit. It varies by state and type of claim. Written contracts typically have 4-6 years, oral agreements 2-4 years, and property damage 2-3 years. Once the deadline passes, you lose the right to sue.

When does the statute of limitations start?

The clock typically starts on the date the breach or injury occurred — when the contract was broken, when the damage happened, or when the last payment was due. In some cases, it starts when you discover the harm.

Can the statute of limitations be extended?

In certain situations, yes. The clock may pause if the defendant leaves the state, if you were a minor when the incident occurred, or if the defendant concealed the wrongdoing. These are called tolling provisions.

What happens if I file after the statute of limitations expires?

The defendant can raise the expired statute as a defense, and the court will dismiss your case. This is why it is critical to know your deadline and file well before it expires.

Does the statute of limitations apply to judgments?

Judgments have their own time limits, typically 5-20 years. Most states allow you to renew a judgment before it expires, effectively giving you unlimited time to collect.
