Tokyo for Under $100 a Day: It's Possible

Tokyo has a reputation as one of the world's most expensive cities, but that reputation is outdated. With the yen remaining favorable for Western travelers in 2026, Tokyo is genuinely affordable if you know the local tricks. Here is how to enjoy the world's greatest city for under $100 per day including accommodation.

Accommodation: $25-50/Night

Capsule Hotels ($25-35): A uniquely Japanese experience. Modern capsule hotels like Nine Hours and First Cabin offer clean, private sleeping pods with shared bathrooms. Perfect for solo travelers.

Business Hotels ($40-50): Compact but private. Toyoko Inn and APA Hotels offer reliable rooms starting around $40. They include free breakfast (rice, miso soup, eggs) and are near every major train station.

Hostels ($20-30): Backpacker hostels in Asakusa and Taito offer dorm beds from $20. Many have excellent common areas and organized activities.

Food: $15-25/Day

Convenience Stores ($3-5/meal): Japanese konbini (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) serve genuinely excellent food. Onigiri ($1.50), bento boxes ($4-5), and sandwiches ($2-3) are fresh and delicious. This is not gas station food.

Standing Ramen Shops ($6-9): Ramen at standing counters (tachigui) serves the same quality as sit-down restaurants for 30-40% less. Order from the ticket machine, eat, leave. Efficient and delicious.

Gyudon Chains ($4-6): Yoshinoya, Sukiya, and Matsuya serve hearty beef bowl sets for under $5. Available 24/7 and scattered throughout every neighborhood.

Depachika ($5-8): Department store basement food halls discount bento boxes and prepared foods by 20-50% in the final hour before closing (typically 7-8 PM). High-end food at convenience store prices.

Transportation: $5-15/Day

Get a Suica or Pasmo IC card at any station for seamless payment across all trains, buses, and convenience stores. A 24-hour Tokyo Metro pass costs just ¥600 ($4.50) for unlimited subway rides. Walking between neighborhoods is also surprisingly pleasant and safe.

The Japan Rail Pass is worth it only if you are taking bullet trains to other cities. For Tokyo-only trips, individual fares or day passes are cheaper.

Free and Cheap Attractions

  • Senso-ji Temple, Asakusa: Free. Tokyo's oldest temple with a stunning approach through Nakamise shopping street.
  • Meiji Shrine, Harajuku: Free. A peaceful forest shrine in the heart of the city.
  • Shibuya Crossing: Free. Watch the world's busiest intersection from the Starbucks above (just buy a coffee).
  • Tsukiji Outer Market: Free to browse. Street food samples from $1-3.
  • Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building: Free observation deck with panoramic views rivaling the $20 Tokyo Skytree tickets.
  • Imperial Palace East Gardens: Free. Beautiful traditional gardens in the heart of Tokyo.
  • TeamLab Borderless: ¥3,800 ($28) but worth the splurge as a once-in-a-lifetime digital art experience.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Tokyo expensive to visit?

Not anymore. With favorable exchange rates in 2026, budget travelers can enjoy Tokyo for $50-95 per day including accommodation, food, transport, and activities.

What is the cheapest food in Tokyo?

Convenience stores (konbini) offer excellent meals for $3-5. Standing ramen shops cost $6-9 and gyudon beef bowl chains serve full meals for $4-6.

What free things can you do in Tokyo?

Senso-ji Temple, Meiji Shrine, Shibuya Crossing, Tsukiji Outer Market, Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building observation deck, and Imperial Palace East Gardens are all free.

How much is public transport in Tokyo?

A 24-hour Tokyo Metro pass costs about $4.50. Individual subway rides cost $1.50-3. Get a Suica or Pasmo IC card for convenient tap-and-go payment.
