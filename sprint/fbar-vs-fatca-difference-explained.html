FBAR vs FATCA: What's the Difference? (Simple Explanation)

Two Reports, Two Agencies, Two Sets of Rules

If you are a US person with money in foreign accounts, you may need to file two separate reports: FBAR and FATCA. They sound similar, cover similar things, and even have overlapping information — but they go to different agencies, have different thresholds, and carry different penalties. Here is the plain-English breakdown.

FBAR: The Basics

FBAR stands for Foreign Bank Account Report (officially FinCEN Form 114). You must file if the aggregate value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeded $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. This means if you had three accounts with $4,000 each, you hit the $12,000 aggregate threshold and must file.

FBAR covers: bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual funds, and certain retirement or pension accounts outside the US. It does not cover foreign real estate (unless held in a foreign account), foreign currency holdings, or precious metals held directly.

FATCA: The Basics

FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) requires reporting on Form 8938 if your foreign financial assets exceed the threshold. FATCA covers a broader range of assets than FBAR: everything FBAR covers plus foreign stock, securities, partnership interests, financial instruments, and contracts with foreign entities.

The threshold is higher: $50,000 at year-end or $75,000 at any time during the year (for US residents). If you live abroad, the thresholds double to $200,000/$300,000.

Who Needs to File What?

  • Foreign accounts totaling $10,000-$49,999: File FBAR only
  • Foreign accounts totaling $50,000+: File both FBAR and FATCA
  • Foreign accounts under $10,000: Neither required (but keep records)
  • Foreign assets but no accounts (e.g., foreign stock held by US broker): May need FATCA only if above threshold

How to File Each Report

FBAR Filing

File electronically at fincen.gov/bsa-e-filing. It is free. You need: account numbers, bank names and addresses, maximum account values during the year. Takes about 20 minutes if you have your statements ready.

FATCA Filing

Complete Form 8938 and attach it to your annual tax return (Form 1040). If you use tax software, it will prompt you. You need: account details, maximum values, income earned in foreign accounts.

What If You Did Not File in Previous Years?

The IRS offers the Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures for people who were non-willfully non-compliant. You file 3 years of amended tax returns and 6 years of FBARs with no penalties. This program has been a lifeline for thousands of expats and dual citizens who did not know about these requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between FBAR and FATCA?

FBAR goes to Treasury when foreign accounts exceed $10,000. FATCA goes to IRS with your tax return when foreign assets exceed $50,000 ($200,000 abroad). Different agencies, thresholds, and forms.

Do I need to file both?

If foreign accounts exceed $50,000, file both. Between $10,000 and $50,000, file FBAR only.

What are the penalties for not filing FBAR?

Up to $10,000 per violation for non-willful. Up to $100,000 or 50% of balance for willful violations.

When is the FBAR deadline?

April 15 with automatic extension to October 15. No separate extension request needed.

What counts as a foreign account?

Bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual funds, and certain retirement accounts outside the US. The $10,000 threshold is based on aggregate value of all foreign accounts combined.
