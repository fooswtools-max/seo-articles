Tax Deductions for Remote Workers Abroad — 2026 Complete Guide

Discover every tax deduction available to US remote workers living abroad in 2026. Covers FEIE, FTC, home office, travel, and digital nomad tax strategies.

US Tax Obligations When Working Remotely Abroad

If you are a US citizen or permanent resident working remotely from another country, you are still required to file US taxes on your worldwide income. The good news: the US tax code offers several powerful exclusions and deductions that can dramatically reduce or eliminate your tax bill.

Understanding these provisions can save you $20,000 to $50,000+ per year in taxes.

The Big Three: FEIE, FTC, and FHEC

1. Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) — Form 2555

The FEIE is the most valuable tax benefit for Americans abroad. In 2026, you can exclude up to approximately $130,000 of earned income from US taxes. This means if you earn $130,000 or less, you may owe zero federal income tax.

To qualify, you must meet one of two tests:

  • Physical Presence Test: Be present in a foreign country for 330 full days during any 12-month period
  • Bona Fide Residence Test: Be a bona fide resident of a foreign country for a full calendar year

2. Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) — Form 1116

If you pay income taxes in your host country, the FTC lets you offset your US tax bill dollar-for-dollar. This prevents double taxation. You can sometimes use FTC for income above the FEIE limit.

3. Foreign Housing Exclusion/Deduction (FHEC)

In addition to the FEIE, you can exclude or deduct housing expenses above a base amount. This covers rent, utilities, insurance, and other housing costs in your foreign city. The exclusion limit varies by location — expensive cities like London and Singapore have higher limits.

Deductions Specifically for Remote Workers Abroad

Home Office Deduction (Self-Employed Only)

If you work as a freelancer or run your own business, you can deduct your foreign home office:

  • Simplified method: $5 per square foot, maximum 300 sq ft = $1,500
  • Regular method: Actual percentage of rent, utilities, internet, and maintenance

Business Travel and Coworking

  • Coworking space memberships (100% deductible for self-employed)
  • Business-related travel between client locations
  • Professional development courses and conferences
  • Computer equipment, software, and internet costs

Health Insurance (Self-Employed)

Self-employed individuals can deduct health insurance premiums, including international health insurance plans. This is an above-the-line deduction, meaning it reduces your adjusted gross income.

Record-Keeping Essentials

Keep meticulous records when working abroad:

  • Travel diary documenting which country you were in each day
  • Copies of all flight tickets, hotel bookings, and visa stamps
  • Foreign rental lease agreement
  • Records of foreign taxes paid
  • All business expense receipts

Frequently Asked Questions

Do US citizens working remotely abroad still pay US taxes?

Yes. The US taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. However, the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) lets you exclude up to $130,000 of foreign earned income from US taxes in 2026, and the Foreign Tax Credit prevents double taxation.

What is the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion for 2026?

The FEIE for 2026 allows you to exclude up to approximately $130,000 of earned income from US taxes. To qualify, you must have a tax home in a foreign country and meet either the bona fide residence test or the physical presence test (330 days abroad in a 12-month period).

Can I deduct my home office if I work remotely abroad?

Yes, if you are self-employed. The home office deduction applies to a dedicated workspace in your foreign residence. You can use the simplified method ($5 per square foot, max 300 sq ft = $1,500) or the regular method tracking actual expenses.

What happens if I work from multiple countries?

You can still claim the FEIE as long as you meet the physical presence test (330 days outside the US) or bona fide residence test. Days in the US count against your physical presence days. Keep detailed records of which country you were in on each date.

Do digital nomads need to pay taxes in the countries they visit?

It depends on the country and how long you stay. Most countries have a 183-day rule — if you stay fewer than 183 days, you are generally not considered a tax resident. However, some countries have different rules. Always research the specific tax treaties between the US and your host countries.
