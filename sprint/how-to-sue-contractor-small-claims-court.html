How to Sue a Contractor in Small Claims Court — 2026 Guide

Step-by-step guide to suing a contractor in small claims court. Learn what evidence you need, how to calculate damages, and how to win your case.

When a Contractor Does Bad Work or Disappears

Hiring a contractor that does shoddy work, abandons the project, or overcharges you is frustrating — but you have legal options. Small claims court is specifically designed for disputes like these, and you do not need a lawyer to win.

Whether your contractor used cheap materials, left the job unfinished, or caused damage to your property, this guide shows you exactly how to hold them accountable.

Before You File: Try to Resolve It

  1. Document everything. Take detailed photos of all work, save every text message and email, and keep all receipts and contracts.
  2. Send a written complaint. Give the contractor a chance to fix the problem. Send a certified letter describing the issues and requesting specific remedies within 14 days.
  3. Get repair estimates. Have 2-3 other licensed contractors provide written estimates to fix or complete the work. These become key evidence in court.
  4. Check their license. Search your state's contractor licensing board. If they are unlicensed, you may be entitled to additional damages and penalties.

How to Calculate Your Damages

The amount you sue for should include:

  • Cost to repair defective work: Based on estimates from other contractors
  • Cost to complete unfinished work: What it will cost another contractor to finish
  • Overpayment: Any money paid beyond the value of work actually completed
  • Consequential damages: Hotel costs, storage fees, rental equipment — anything directly caused by the contractor's failure
  • Court costs: Filing fees, service fees, and time off work

Pro Tip: Make sure your total claim is within your state's small claims court limit. If your damages exceed the limit, you can either waive the excess amount to stay in small claims court, or file in regular civil court.

Filing Your Case

File in the county where the work was performed or where the contractor's business is located. You will need:

  • The contractor's full legal name and business name (check the license or contract)
  • The contractor's business address for service of process
  • A clear description of the dispute and amount claimed
  • Copies of contracts, receipts, and photos organized chronologically

Winning Your Case in Court

Contractor cases are won with evidence. Bring everything organized in a binder:

  1. The contract or written agreement showing what was promised
  2. Payment records showing what you paid
  3. Photos of defective work with dates and descriptions
  4. Repair estimates from other licensed contractors
  5. Communication records showing your attempts to resolve the issue
  6. Your demand letter and proof it was sent

Present your case as a clear timeline: what was agreed, what was paid, what went wrong, and what it will cost to fix. Judges appreciate organized plaintiffs who stick to the facts.

After the Judgment

If you win, the contractor has a set number of days to pay (usually 30). If they do not pay, you can use collection tools like wage garnishment, bank account levies, or property liens to enforce the judgment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sue a contractor in small claims court?

Yes. Small claims court is ideal for contractor disputes involving shoddy work, unfinished projects, or overcharging. Most states allow claims up to $5,000-$20,000, which covers many home improvement disputes.

What evidence do I need to sue a contractor?

You need the written contract or agreement, all payments made with receipts, photos of the work before and after, any text messages or emails discussing the project, estimates from other contractors to fix the work, and proof of the contractor's license.

How much can I sue a contractor for?

You can sue for the cost to repair or complete the work, the difference between what you paid and the value received, any related damages like hotel costs if your home was uninhabitable, and potentially punitive damages if the contractor was unlicensed.

Should I report the contractor to the licensing board too?

Yes. Filing a complaint with your state's contractor licensing board is separate from your small claims case but can help. An unlicensed contractor may face additional penalties, and some states have recovery funds that pay victims of unlicensed contractors.

What if the contractor filed a lien on my property?

If a contractor filed a mechanics lien, you may need to address this in regular court rather than small claims. However, you can still file a small claims case for damages while the lien dispute is handled separately.
