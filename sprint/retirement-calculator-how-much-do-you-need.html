The Simple Formula: 25x Your Annual Expenses

Forget complicated retirement calculators with 30 input fields. The core math is simple: multiply your annual expenses by 25. That is your retirement number. Spend $40,000 per year? You need $1 million. Spend $60,000? You need $1.5 million.

This is based on the 4 percent rule: withdraw 4 percent of your portfolio in year one, then adjust for inflation. Historical data shows this strategy has a 95 percent success rate over 30-year retirement periods.

Do Not Forget Social Security

Social Security reduces the amount you need to save. The average Social Security benefit in 2026 is about $1,900 per month ($22,800/year). If your expenses are $50,000 per year and Social Security covers $22,800, you only need to cover $27,200 from savings. That means $680,000 instead of $1.25 million.

Check your estimated benefit at ssa.gov/myaccount. Your actual benefit depends on your earnings history and the age you start claiming.

The Three Retirement Accounts You Should Max Out

  • 401(k) or 403(b) — contribute at least enough to get the full employer match (free money). 2026 limit: $23,500 ($31,000 if over 50)
  • Roth IRA — tax-free growth and withdrawals. 2026 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if over 50)
  • HSA (if eligible) — triple tax advantage. 2026 limit: $4,300 individual, $8,550 family

Can You Retire Early?

The FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) movement proves early retirement is possible. The math is the same — 25x expenses — but you need to account for more years of withdrawals. Many early retirees use a 3.5 percent withdrawal rate instead of 4 percent for extra safety.

To retire at 50 with $40,000 annual expenses: save $1.14 million (at 3.5% rate) and bridge the gap to Social Security at 62 or 67 with your portfolio.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much money do I need to retire?

25 times your annual expenses. Spend $50,000/year, you need $1.25 million. Social Security reduces this number.

What is the 4 percent rule?

Withdraw 4 percent in year one, adjust for inflation each year. A $1M portfolio provides $40,000/year with a 95% historical success rate over 30 years.

How much should I save monthly?

At least 15% of gross income. Starting at 30, $550/month at 7% returns grows to about $1 million by 65.

Can I retire with $500,000?

$500K provides $20,000/year. Combined with Social Security ($22,800 average), total income is about $42,800/year — workable in lower cost areas.

At what age can I retire?

Whenever your savings hit 25x annual expenses. Traditional age is 65-67 for full Social Security. Early retirement at 50-55 is possible with aggressive saving.
