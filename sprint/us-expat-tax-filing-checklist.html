US Expat Tax Filing Checklist 2026 — Everything You Need to File

The Complete US Expat Tax Filing Checklist

If you are a US citizen or green card holder living abroad, you are required to file US taxes on your worldwide income regardless of where you live. The good news: several exclusions and credits prevent most expats from owing anything. The bad news: the paperwork is confusing. This checklist covers everything.

Key Deadlines for 2026

  • April 15, 2026: Standard US tax filing deadline (automatic 2-month extension to June 15 for expats)
  • June 15, 2026: Automatic expat extension deadline
  • October 15, 2026: Extended deadline (must file Form 4868 by April 15)
  • April 15, 2026: FBAR deadline (automatic extension to October 15)

Income Reporting Checklist

You must report ALL worldwide income including:

  • Foreign employment income (salary, bonuses, benefits)
  • Self-employment and freelance income earned abroad
  • Rental income from properties in any country
  • Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)
  • Pension and retirement distributions (foreign and US)
  • Bank interest from foreign accounts

Key Tax Forms for Expats

Form 1040: Your standard US individual tax return, required for all US citizens and green card holders.

Form 2555 (Foreign Earned Income Exclusion): Excludes up to $126,500 (2026) of foreign earned income from US taxation. You must pass either the Physical Presence Test (330 days abroad in a 12-month period) or the Bona Fide Residence Test.

Form 1116 (Foreign Tax Credit): Credits taxes paid to your country of residence against your US tax liability. Often more beneficial than the FEIE for higher earners. You can claim both, but not on the same income.

FBAR (FinCEN 114): Required if total foreign financial accounts exceed $10,000 at any point during the year. Filed electronically through the BSA E-Filing System. Penalties for non-filing: up to $10,000 per account per year for non-willful violations.

Form 8938 (FATCA): Required if foreign financial assets exceed $200,000 (end of year) or $300,000 (any time) for expats filing individually. Higher thresholds than FBAR but filed with your tax return.

FEIE vs Foreign Tax Credit: Which to Choose?

This is the most important decision for expat tax optimization:

  • Choose FEIE if you live in a low-tax or no-tax country (UAE, Singapore, Hong Kong)
  • Choose Foreign Tax Credit if you live in a high-tax country (most of Europe, Israel, Japan, Australia)
  • Use both if you have income from multiple sources (exclude salary with FEIE, credit investment income taxes with FTC)

Common Expat Mistakes

  • Not filing at all (the IRS knows about your foreign accounts through FATCA reporting by banks)
  • Missing the FBAR filing requirement (separate from your tax return, separate deadline)
  • Choosing FEIE when FTC would save more money
  • Not reporting foreign pension contributions or distributions
  • Ignoring state tax obligations (some states continue to tax you after you leave)

Streamlined Filing for Late Filers

If you have not been filing, the IRS Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures let you catch up by filing 3 years of tax returns and 6 years of FBARs with no penalties if your failure was non-willful. This is a legitimate amnesty program that thousands of expats use successfully every year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do US citizens living abroad have to file taxes?

Yes. US citizens and green card holders must file US taxes on worldwide income regardless of where they live. The filing threshold is the same as for domestic filers.

What is the FBAR filing requirement?

If your total foreign financial accounts exceed $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file FBAR (FinCEN 114) electronically by April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15.

What is the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion for 2026?

The FEIE allows you to exclude up to $126,500 of foreign earned income from US taxation if you pass the Physical Presence Test (330 days abroad) or Bona Fide Residence Test.

What happens if I haven't filed US taxes from abroad?

The IRS Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures allow you to catch up by filing 3 years of returns and 6 years of FBARs with no penalties for non-willful non-compliance.
