Small Claims Court Evidence Guide: What Judges Want to See in 2026

Learn exactly what evidence wins small claims court cases. Covers photos, contracts, texts, receipts, witnesses, and how to organize your case file for court day.

Why Evidence Wins Small Claims Cases

In small claims court, the person with the best evidence almost always wins. Judges make decisions based on what you can prove, not just what you say. A well-organized evidence package can be the difference between winning your full claim and walking away with nothing.

This guide covers exactly what types of evidence judges find most persuasive and how to organize your case for maximum impact.

The Evidence Hierarchy: What Judges Value Most

  1. Signed contracts or written agreements — The gold standard. If you have a signed contract that the other party violated, you are in a strong position.
  2. Payment records — Bank statements, canceled checks, credit card statements, receipts, and invoices showing what you paid.
  3. Written communications — Emails, text messages, and letters that show what was agreed upon, promised, or admitted.
  4. Photographic and video evidence — Before and after photos, damage documentation, condition of work performed.
  5. Third-party estimates — Written quotes from professionals showing the cost to repair or complete deficient work.
  6. Witness testimony — People who directly observed the relevant events.

How to Prepare Each Type of Evidence

Contracts and Written Agreements

Make 3 clear copies of the complete contract, including any addendums or change orders. Highlight the specific clauses that were violated. If the agreement was verbal, write down exactly what was agreed upon, including dates, amounts, and who was present.

Payment Records

Create a clear timeline of every payment made. Include bank statements highlighting the transactions, copies of canceled checks (front and back), credit card statements, and any receipts. Calculate the total amount paid vs. the value of work actually received.

Text Messages and Emails

Screenshots of text messages are powerful evidence. Print full conversation threads, not just cherry-picked messages. Include:

  • The contact name and phone number visible in the screenshot
  • Dates and times of messages
  • Complete conversation context (do not crop out messages that do not help your case — judges notice this)

Photos and Videos

Organize photos chronologically with clear labels:

  • Date the photo was taken
  • What the photo shows
  • Location or angle

Print photos in color, one or two per page, large enough for the judge to see clearly. For videos, bring a fully charged tablet or laptop.

Repair Estimates

Get 2-3 written estimates from licensed professionals to fix or complete the deficient work. These should be on company letterhead, itemized, and dated. This establishes the dollar value of your damages.

Organizing Your Court File

The 3-Copy Rule: Always bring three identical copies of your entire evidence package — one for yourself, one for the judge, and one for the opposing party. Use a binder or folder with labeled tabs for each evidence category. Number every page.

Common Evidence Mistakes That Lose Cases

  • Not bringing originals: Bring original documents when possible, with copies as backup.
  • Disorganized presentation: Fumbling through loose papers wastes the judge's limited time and patience.
  • Emotional statements instead of facts: Stick to dates, amounts, and documented facts. Save the frustration for private conversations.
  • Missing payment proof: If you cannot prove you paid, you cannot prove you lost money.
  • Not calculating damages clearly: Present a simple one-page summary showing: amount paid minus value received equals damages claimed.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most important evidence in small claims court?

Written agreements and contracts are the strongest evidence. After that, receipts showing payment, photos documenting damage or defective work, and written communications (texts, emails) that prove what was agreed upon.

Can I use text messages as evidence in small claims court?

Yes. Text messages are admissible evidence in small claims court. Print screenshots showing the full conversation thread, including phone numbers and dates. Judges consider texts very persuasive because people are candid in text messages.

Do I need a witness for small claims court?

Witnesses are helpful but not required. If you have a witness who saw the incident or can testify about the quality of work, bring them. If they cannot attend, a signed written statement (declaration) can sometimes be submitted instead.

How should I organize my evidence for court?

Create a labeled binder or folder with tabs: Tab 1 - Contract/Agreement, Tab 2 - Payment Records, Tab 3 - Photos/Videos, Tab 4 - Communications, Tab 5 - Repair Estimates, Tab 6 - Witness Statements. Make 3 copies: one for you, one for the judge, one for the defendant.

Can I bring photos and videos to small claims court?

Yes. Print photos in color on standard paper, clearly labeled with dates. For videos, bring a tablet or laptop to show the judge. Some courts also accept USB drives. Always bring printed photo backups in case technology fails.
