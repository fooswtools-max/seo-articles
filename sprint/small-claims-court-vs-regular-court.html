Small Claims Court vs Regular Court: Which Do You Need?

Understand the key differences between small claims court and regular civil court. Learn which is right for your case based on amount, complexity, and timeline.

Two Courts, Very Different Experiences

If someone owes you money or caused you financial harm, you need to choose the right court. Small claims court and regular civil court serve different purposes, and choosing the wrong one can waste your time and money.

This guide breaks down the differences so you can make the right choice for your situation.

When to Choose Small Claims Court

  • Your claim is under your state's dollar limit
  • The facts are straightforward (unpaid debt, property damage, deposit dispute)
  • You do not want to hire a lawyer
  • You want a fast resolution (weeks, not years)
  • You can present your case with documents, photos, and basic testimony

When You Need Regular Court

  • Your claim exceeds the small claims limit and you do not want to waive the excess
  • The case involves complex legal issues (intellectual property, class actions, corporate disputes)
  • You need formal discovery to obtain evidence from the other party
  • You need an injunction or restraining order (not just money)
  • The case involves family law, probate, or criminal matters

Strategy Tip: If your claim is slightly over the small claims limit, consider waiving the excess to stay in small claims court. The savings in time, legal fees, and stress often outweigh the small amount you give up.

The Cost Difference Is Massive

A typical small claims case costs $50-150 total (filing fee plus service). A regular civil court case with an attorney can cost $5,000-50,000 or more in legal fees alone, even for a straightforward dispute. For most everyday money disputes, small claims court delivers the same result at a fraction of the cost.

What About Mediation?

Many courts offer free or low-cost mediation as an alternative. A neutral mediator helps both parties reach an agreement without going before a judge. Mediation has a high success rate and is available in both small claims and regular court settings. Some courts require you to try mediation before scheduling a hearing.

Making Your Decision

If your dispute is about money, the amount is within the limit, and the facts are clear, small claims court is almost always the right choice. It is faster, cheaper, and designed for regular people to represent themselves successfully.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main difference between small claims and regular court?

Small claims court handles disputes under a specific dollar limit (varies by state from $2,500 to $25,000), uses simplified procedures, does not require lawyers, and resolves cases in weeks rather than months or years. Regular court handles larger or more complex cases with formal legal procedures.

Do I need a lawyer for small claims court?

No. Small claims court is designed for self-representation. In some states like California, attorneys are not even allowed to represent parties. You present your own case directly to the judge.

What types of cases can go to small claims court?

Small claims court handles money disputes including unpaid debts, property damage, security deposit disputes, breach of contract, defective products, and auto accident damage. It generally cannot handle family law, evictions, or injunctions.

Can I appeal a small claims court decision?

In most states, yes. The losing party can appeal to a higher court within 30 days. However, some states only allow the defendant to appeal if the case was decided without them being present.

How long does a small claims case take?

From filing to hearing is typically 30 to 60 days. The hearing itself lasts 15 to 30 minutes. Compare this to regular civil court which can take 1 to 3 years or longer to reach trial.
