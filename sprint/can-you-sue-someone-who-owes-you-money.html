Can You Sue Someone Who Owes You Money? A Complete Guide

Someone owes you money and won't pay? Learn when and how to sue in small claims court, what evidence you need, and how to collect after winning your case.

Yes, You Can Sue — And You Should

When someone borrows money and refuses to pay it back, you have every right to take them to court. Small claims court was created exactly for situations like this — it is fast, affordable, and you do not need a lawyer.

Whether it is a friend who borrowed $500, a customer who never paid an invoice, or a roommate who skipped out on rent, this guide covers everything you need to know about recovering your money through the legal system.

When Is Suing Worth It?

Consider these factors before filing:

  • Amount owed vs. filing costs: If the debt is under $100, the filing fee alone may not make it worthwhile
  • Can the person pay? A judgment against someone with no income or assets may be uncollectable
  • Do you have evidence? Text messages, emails, contracts, bank transfers, or witnesses
  • Is it within the statute of limitations? Check your state's deadline for filing debt claims

What Evidence Do You Need?

The stronger your evidence, the better your chances. Gather:

  • Written agreement or contract — even an informal text like "I'll pay you back next month" counts
  • Bank or payment records showing the money transfer (Venmo, Zelle, checks, cash withdrawal)
  • Text messages and emails where the person acknowledges the debt
  • Witness statements from anyone who saw the transaction or heard the agreement
  • Invoices or receipts for services rendered or goods delivered

Key Tip: Screenshot all text messages and save them to your computer or email. Text messages on phones can be lost, deleted, or become inaccessible. Print physical copies for court.

How to File Your Case

  1. Send a demand letter first. This is often required and shows the judge you tried to resolve it. Send via certified mail with a 14-day payment deadline.
  2. Identify the correct court. File in the county where the defendant lives or where the transaction took place.
  3. Complete the filing forms. Most courts have simple forms available online. You will need the defendant's full name, address, the amount owed, and a brief description of the dispute.
  4. Pay the filing fee. Fees range from $15-200 depending on your state and claim amount.
  5. Serve the defendant. The court papers must be officially delivered to the defendant, usually by a process server, sheriff, or certified mail.

At the Hearing

Small claims hearings are informal and typically last 15-30 minutes. Present your evidence in order: show the agreement, prove the money was given, demonstrate attempts to collect, and present your demand letter. Let the evidence speak for itself.

Collecting After You Win

Winning the judgment is step one. If the defendant does not pay voluntarily, you can:

  • Garnish their wages — the employer sends a portion of their paycheck to you
  • Levy their bank account — the sheriff freezes and takes money from their account
  • Place a lien on their property — they cannot sell without paying you first
  • Use a collection agency — they take a percentage but handle the enforcement

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sue someone for money they owe me?

Yes. If someone owes you money and refuses to pay, you can file a claim in small claims court. You need evidence of the debt such as a written agreement, text messages, bank transfers, or witnesses who can confirm the loan.

How much does it cost to sue someone who owes me money?

Small claims court filing fees range from $15 to $200 depending on your state and the amount claimed. If you win, the court can order the defendant to reimburse your filing fee.

Can I sue without a written contract?

Yes. Oral agreements are legally enforceable in most situations. You will need other evidence to prove the agreement existed, such as text messages, emails, witness testimony, or bank records showing the money transfer.

What is the statute of limitations for debt collection?

It varies by state and type of debt. Written contracts typically have 4-6 year statutes of limitations. Oral agreements are usually 2-4 years. Once the statute expires, you cannot sue to collect.

What if the person lives in a different state?

You generally need to file in the state and county where the defendant lives. Some states allow filing where the transaction occurred. For small amounts, the travel costs may make cross-state cases impractical.
