Amsterdam in Winter: Why Off-Season Is Actually Better

Updated April 2026 · 8 min read

Everyone visits Amsterdam in summer. The canals sparkle, the parks bloom, and the streets overflow with tourists. But winter Amsterdam — November through February — has something summer can't offer: the city actually feels like it belongs to you. Here's why the cold season might be the best time to go.

The Case for Winter Amsterdam

Museums Without the Madness

In summer, the Van Gogh Museum line wraps around the block. The Anne Frank House sells out weeks in advance. The Rijksmuseum is shoulder-to-shoulder. In winter? Walk into the Van Gogh Museum at 2pm on a Tuesday and stand alone with Starry Night. Anne Frank House tickets are available same-week. The Rijksmuseum's Night Watch gallery is practically private.

Hotel Prices Drop 40-50%

That boutique hotel on the Herengracht that costs €300/night in July? €140-180 in January. Budget hotels in the Jordaan drop to €80-100. The money you save on accommodation more than covers warm drinks and indoor activities.

The "Gezelligheid" Factor

The Dutch have a word — gezellig — that roughly translates to cozy, warm, convivial. It's the feeling of sitting in a candlelit brown café while rain hits the windows, nursing a Dutch beer, and watching boats pass on the canal. Winter Amsterdam is peak gezelligheid. This feeling simply doesn't exist in summer.

What to Do in Winter Amsterdam

Amsterdam Light Festival (November-January)

Light art installations along the canals by international artists. Walk the route for free or take an evening canal cruise to see them from the water. This alone is worth timing your visit for.

Brown Cafés (Bruine Kroegen)

Amsterdam's traditional pubs, called brown cafés for their dark wood interiors. They're perfect in winter. Try:

  • Café 't Smalle — 18th-century tasting house on the Egelantiersgracht. Stunning canal-side terrace (covered and heated in winter).
  • Café Papeneiland — Since 1642. Famous for apple pie.
  • Café De Dokter — Amsterdam's smallest café. Seats about 8 people.

Indoor Markets

Foodhallen in Oud-West is a food market in a former tram depot — bitterballen, Vietnamese pho, craft beer, and a warm atmosphere. Albert Cuyp Market stays open year-round with warm stroopwafels and poffertjes (mini pancakes).

The Jordaan in Snow

When it snows (1-3 times per winter), the Jordaan neighborhood transforms into a postcard. Bicycles covered in snow along frozen canals, warm light from café windows, and that particular silence that snow brings to a city. If you're lucky enough to catch a snow day, walk the Jordaan and Prinsengracht.

Practical Winter Tips

  • Layer up — Amsterdam's winter is 0-7°C with persistent dampness and wind. Waterproof jacket, warm layers, and a good scarf are essential.
  • Daylight hours — In December, sunrise is around 8:30am and sunset at 4:30pm. Plan outdoor activities for midday.
  • Rain is constant — It doesn't pour, but it drizzles. Often. Carry a compact umbrella or embrace getting slightly damp like the Dutch do.
  • Cycling is year-round — Amsterdammers cycle in all weather. Rental bikes are available but wet cobblestones are slippery. Walk if you're not a confident cyclist.
  • Book Anne Frank House early — Even in winter, it sells out. Tickets release online 6 weeks ahead at 10am. Set an alarm.

Is Amsterdam depressing in winter?

No, but it requires a different mindset. If you need sunshine and outdoor dining, wait for summer. If you appreciate cozy interiors, quiet museums, and atmospheric walks, winter Amsterdam is genuinely wonderful.

Do the canals freeze in winter?

Rarely these days, maybe once every few years. When they do, it's a national event and everyone goes skating. Don't plan on it, but if it happens during your visit, it's unforgettable.

What should I pack for winter Amsterdam?

Waterproof jacket, warm sweater, scarf, waterproof shoes or boots, gloves, and a compact umbrella. Dress for 0-7°C with wind and rain. Layers are key since museums and cafés are warm inside.

Are tulip gardens open in winter?

Keukenhof opens mid-March, so you'll miss it in deep winter. However, you can see tulips at the floating flower market (Bloemenmarkt) year-round. February visits catch the earliest blooms.
