Bali Temple Etiquette: What Every Tourist Must Know

Updated April 2026 · 8 min read

Bali has over 20,000 temples, and they're not museums or tourist attractions — they're active places of worship where Balinese Hindus pray daily. The most beautiful experiences in Bali happen when you visit temples respectfully. Here's everything you need to know to do it right.

The Golden Rules

  1. Wear a sarong and sash — Required at every temple. A sarong (or selendang) covers your legs, and a sash ties around your waist. Most major temples provide them at the entrance (sometimes free, sometimes 10,000-20,000 IDR rental). Carrying your own is more respectful and hygienic.
  2. Cover your shoulders — No tank tops, sleeveless shirts, or bikini tops. A t-shirt is fine. If you forgot, sarongs can be wrapped over shoulders too.
  3. Women on their period should not enter — This is a deeply held Balinese Hindu belief. Menstruating women are considered "ritually unclean" and are not permitted in temples. Signs at temple entrances state this. Respecting this rule, even if you disagree with the reasoning, is essential.
  4. Don't stand higher than the priest or offerings — If a ceremony is happening, stay at or below the level of the proceedings. Don't climb on walls or structures for photos.
  5. Never touch offerings — The small flower baskets (canang sari) placed everywhere are offerings to Hindu gods. Don't step on them, move them, or pick them up.
Critical rule: If a ceremony is in progress, you may be asked to sit or wait. Always comply. You are a guest in someone's place of worship. The ceremony takes absolute priority over your sightseeing schedule.

At the Temple: Behavior Guide

  • Ask before photographing people — Photographing the temple architecture is fine. Photographing people praying requires permission. Many will say yes, but always ask.
  • Walk behind, not in front of people who are praying — Just as you wouldn't walk in front of someone at church, don't cross between a praying person and the shrine.
  • Use your right hand for giving, receiving, and pointing. The left hand is considered unclean in Balinese culture.
  • Speak quietly — Temples are sacred spaces. Keep conversation low and avoid shouting or laughing loudly.
  • Don't point at sacred objects with your index finger. Use your whole hand or thumb instead.
  • Remove shoes if you see others doing so, or if signs indicate. Some inner sanctums require barefoot entry.

Major Temples and Specific Rules

Tanah Lot

The iconic ocean temple. Non-Hindus cannot enter the temple itself but can walk around the rock formation and watch sunset. Arrive 1 hour before sunset for the best experience. Avoid the aggressive souvenir sellers on the path down.

Uluwatu Temple

Clifftop temple famous for Kecak dance performances at sunset. Watch your belongings — the monkeys here are notorious thieves. Remove sunglasses, hats, and anything dangling. They will snatch phones and demand food for return. Sarong provided at entrance.

Besakih (Mother Temple)

Bali's most important temple complex on Mount Agung's slopes. Hire an official guide at the ticket office only (many unofficial "guides" will approach you outside). The temple is massive — plan 2-3 hours. Some inner areas are closed to non-Hindus.

Tirta Empul (Holy Water Temple)

Famous for the purification pools where visitors can participate in the ritual bathing. If you choose to do this: follow the pools in order (left to right), skip the two pools reserved for funeral purification (usually marked), and submerge your head under each spout three times. Wear appropriate bathing clothes under your sarong.

Ulun Danu Beratan

The photogenic lake temple. Less strict than some temples since much of the visit is in the surrounding gardens. Still dress appropriately with sarong and sash.

What to Wear: Practical Guide

  • Buy a sarong at any local market for 30,000-80,000 IDR ($2-5). It's a useful souvenir and you'll use it at multiple temples.
  • T-shirt or blouse covering shoulders. Lightweight and breathable since temples get hot.
  • Comfortable walking shoes that are easy to slip on and off. Many temple grounds involve stairs and uneven stone.
  • Sash (selendang) — Usually included with sarong purchase. Yellow or green is traditional.

Beyond Temples: General Cultural Respect

  • Don't step on canang sari (flower offerings) on sidewalks. They're everywhere in Bali. Walk around them.
  • Nyepi (Day of Silence) means the entire island shuts down for 24 hours. No flights, no leaving your hotel, no lights. Check the date before booking (March 2026).
  • Haggling at markets is expected, but start at 50-60% of asking price, not 10%. Aggressive lowballing is disrespectful.
  • Tipping is appreciated but not required. 10-15% at restaurants, round up for drivers.

Can non-Hindus enter Bali temples?

Most temples allow non-Hindu visitors to enter the outer areas. Inner sanctums are often restricted to practicing Hindus, especially during ceremonies. Always follow signage and local guidance.

Do I need to buy a sarong or can I borrow one?

Major temples provide sarong rentals (10,000-20,000 IDR). However, buying your own (30,000-80,000 IDR at any market) is more hygienic, fits better, and makes a great souvenir.

What time should I visit temples?

Early morning (7-9am) for fewer crowds and cooler temperatures. Sunset temples like Tanah Lot and Uluwatu are best visited 1 hour before sunset. Avoid midday heat, especially at hilltop temples.

Is it OK to participate in ceremonies as a tourist?

At Tirta Empul, tourist participation in the purification ritual is welcomed and encouraged. At other temples, you may be invited to receive a blessing — accept it graciously. Always follow instructions from the priest.

Are drones allowed at Bali temples?

Generally no. Most major temples have banned drones. Flying a drone over a temple ceremony is considered extremely disrespectful. Check specific temple rules, but default to leaving it in the hotel.
