FBAR vs FATCA Quick Reference

Key differences between FBAR and FATCA reporting at a glance

ReturnMyTax — returnmytax.com

FBAR and FATCA are both foreign account reporting requirements, but they are different filings with different thresholds, deadlines, and penalties. This guide shows the differences at a glance.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Feature FBAR (FinCEN 114) FATCA (Form 8938)
Who files? US persons with foreign accounts US taxpayers with foreign financial assets
Filed with FinCEN (separate from tax return) IRS (attached to Form 1040)
Filing method Electronic only via BSA E-Filing With your tax return (paper or e-file)
Threshold (US residents) $10,000 aggregate at any point $50,000 (year-end) or $75,000 (any point)
Threshold (expats, single) $10,000 aggregate at any point $200,000 (year-end) or $300,000 (any point)
Threshold (expats, MFJ) $10,000 aggregate at any point $400,000 (year-end) or $600,000 (any point)
What's reported Bank accounts, investment accounts, mutual funds, pensions with account numbers Same as FBAR plus: foreign stock/securities, financial instruments, contracts, insurance
Deadline April 15 (auto-ext to Oct 15) With your tax return deadline
Penalty (non-willful) Up to $10,000 per account per year $10,000 per form
Penalty (willful) $100,000 or 50% of balance $50,000 + criminal prosecution
Do I need both? YES — if you meet both thresholds, you must file both. Filing one does NOT exempt you from the other.

What Counts as a "Foreign Financial Account"?

FBAR Only

Bank accounts (checking, savings) | Securities accounts | Mutual funds | Debit/prepaid card accounts | Insurance policies with cash value | Accounts you have signature authority over (even if not yours)

FATCA Only (in addition to FBAR assets)

Foreign stock or securities not in an account | Foreign partnership interests | Foreign mutual funds (PFICs) | Foreign hedge funds | Foreign-issued life insurance | Foreign pension plans (some) | Financial contracts held for investment

Quick Decision Guide

  • Foreign accounts total exceeded $10,000 at ANY point? File FBAR. This is an aggregate total — if you have 5 accounts with $2,500 each = $12,500 = FBAR required.
  • Foreign financial assets exceed the FATCA threshold? File Form 8938 with your 1040. Expat thresholds are much higher than US-resident thresholds.
  • You have a foreign pension or retirement account? Usually reportable on both FBAR and FATCA, plus possibly Form 3520 if it's a foreign trust.
  • You hold foreign mutual funds? Report on FBAR + FATCA + Form 8621 (PFIC). Consider switching to US-domiciled funds.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Thinking FBAR is part of your tax return — it's filed separately via BSA E-Filing.
Mistake 2: Using year-end balance for FBAR threshold — it's the maximum balance at ANY point during the year.
Mistake 3: Forgetting joint accounts — if you have signature authority, you must report it.
Mistake 4: Filing only one — if you meet both thresholds, both are required.
