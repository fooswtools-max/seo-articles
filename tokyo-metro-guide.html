Tokyo Metro Guide: How to Navigate Like a Local

Updated April 2026 · 9 min read

Tokyo's rail system carries 40 million passengers daily and runs with legendary precision. It's also genuinely confusing for first-timers — multiple operators, hundreds of stations, signs in Japanese, and transfer stations that are underground cities. Here's everything you need to navigate it confidently.

The System: What You're Dealing With

Tokyo doesn't have "a" metro. It has several overlapping systems:

  • Tokyo Metro — 9 lines, identified by letters and colors (G for Ginza Line, M for Marunouchi, etc.)
  • Toei Subway — 4 additional lines, different company but your IC card works on both
  • JR (Japan Railways) — Includes the essential Yamanote Line (the green circle line that connects major hubs)
  • Private railways — Keio, Odakyu, Tokyu, etc. These connect central Tokyo to suburbs and are covered by IC cards
Don't panic: You don't need to understand the system perfectly. Get an IC card, use Google Maps for directions, and tap in and out. The system handles the routing and fare calculation automatically.

Getting a Suica or Pasmo Card

These rechargeable IC cards are essential. They work on ALL trains, buses, and even convenience stores and vending machines:

  • Suica — Issued by JR. Buy from JR ticket machines (look for the penguin logo).
  • Pasmo — Issued by Tokyo Metro/Toei. Functionally identical to Suica.
  • Mobile Suica — Add to Apple Wallet or Google Pay. No physical card needed. This is the best option in 2026.
  • Deposit: 500 yen (refundable). Charge in increments at any station machine.
Important in 2026: Physical Suica/Pasmo cards have limited availability due to chip shortages. Mobile Suica on your phone is the most reliable option. Set it up before arriving.

How to Navigate: Step by Step

  1. Open Google Maps (or the Navitime or Japan Transit apps). Enter your destination. It shows you exactly which line, which platform, which exit.
  2. Follow station numbers. Every station has a letter-number code (e.g., G-09 for Ginza on the Ginza Line). Follow the color-coded signs.
  3. Tap your IC card at the gate. Walk through. The system knows your entry point.
  4. Follow platform signs. They show the direction (terminus station name) and upcoming stops.
  5. Exit and tap out. The system calculates your fare automatically. If you exit at the wrong station, find a fare adjustment machine near the gates.

Etiquette Rules (Taken Seriously)

  • No talking on the phone on trains. Set your phone to silent (manner mode).
  • No eating or drinking on subway trains (long-distance trains like Shinkansen are different).
  • Queue on the platform along the marked lines. Let passengers exit before boarding.
  • Priority seats are for elderly, pregnant, disabled, and injured passengers. Don't sit there during rush hour.
  • Backpacks go in front of you or on the luggage rack when the train is crowded.
  • Women-only cars exist during morning rush (marked in pink). Men should avoid these cars before 9:30am.

Money-Saving Tips

  • Tokyo Subway Ticket — Unlimited Tokyo Metro + Toei rides for 24h (800 yen), 48h (1,200 yen), or 72h (1,500 yen). Excellent value if you're sightseeing intensively. Does NOT cover JR lines.
  • JR Pass — Only worth it if you're also traveling to Kyoto/Osaka. For Tokyo-only trips, skip it.
  • Walk between nearby stations — Many stations in central Tokyo are 5-10 minutes apart on foot. Check before paying for a one-stop ride.
Rush hour warning: 7:30-9:30am trains are packed to 180% capacity. If you can, avoid traveling during this window. Trains after 9:30am are comfortable. Evening rush (5:30-8pm) is busy but less extreme.

Is Tokyo's metro system English-friendly?

Yes. All station names, signs, and announcements are in English (and often Chinese and Korean). The ticket machines have an English language option. Google Maps works perfectly for navigation.

What time does the last train run?

Most last trains depart between 11:30pm and midnight. Missing the last train is expensive — taxis from central Tokyo can cost 5,000-10,000 yen. Check your last train time before going out at night.

How do I get from Narita Airport to central Tokyo?

Narita Express (N'EX) to Tokyo Station takes 60 minutes and costs 3,070 yen. The Skyliner to Ueno/Nippori takes 41 minutes for 2,520 yen. Budget option: Access Express for 1,270 yen but takes 90+ minutes.

Can I use my IC card on buses too?

Yes. Suica and Pasmo work on virtually all buses in Tokyo. Tap when boarding (rear door) and when exiting (front door). The fare is usually a flat 210 yen.
