London on a Budget: Complete 2026 Guide (Under $100/Day)

Updated April 2026 · 9 min read

London has a reputation as one of the world's most expensive cities, and honestly, it can be. But with the right strategy, you can experience world-class museums, incredible food markets, and iconic sights for under $100/day — including accommodation.

Free Museums (Yes, Really Free)

London's greatest budget advantage: its best museums charge nothing. These aren't second-rate collections — they're genuinely world-class:

  • British Museum — The Rosetta Stone, Egyptian mummies, Parthenon sculptures. Plan 3+ hours.
  • Natural History Museum — The building itself is worth visiting. Blue whale skeleton, dinosaur gallery, stunning architecture.
  • Tate Modern — One of the world's best contemporary art museums, housed in a former power station on the Thames.
  • National Gallery — Van Gogh, Monet, da Vinci, Rembrandt. Overlooking Trafalgar Square.
  • V&A Museum — Design, fashion, and decorative arts. The café alone is Instagram-worthy.
  • Science Museum — Interactive exhibits, space exploration, engineering. Great for all ages.
Pro tip: Some free museums have paid special exhibitions. The permanent collections alone are worth full days, so skip the paid shows if you're budgeting.

Cheap Eats That Don't Sacrifice Quality

Forget sit-down restaurants in central London. Here's where locals eat well for less:

  • Borough Market — Yes, it's touristy, but the food quality is real. Ethiopian injera, raclette, fresh oysters for £2 each. Go for lunch, not dinner.
  • Brick Lane — Curry houses compete fiercely on price. £7-9 for a full curry with rice and naan.
  • Chinatown — Dim sum from £4. Bao buns. Roast duck rice for £8. Stick to the busy places.
  • Dishoom — Queue for breakfast (free). Their bacon naan roll (£8) is legendary.
  • Supermarket meal deals — Tesco, Sainsbury's, and M&S offer sandwich + drink + snack for £3.50-5. This is genuinely good food.

Transport Hacks

The Tube is efficient but expensive. Smart strategies:

  • Use contactless payment — Daily and weekly caps mean you never overpay. No need to buy an Oyster card anymore.
  • Walk between nearby stations — Many Tube stations are just 5-10 minutes apart on foot. The walk between Covent Garden and Leicester Square takes 4 minutes.
  • Take buses — £1.75 per ride, capped at £5.25/day. The top deck of a double-decker is free sightseeing.
  • Avoid Zone 1 during peak hours — Before 9:30am, fares are significantly higher.
Best free walk: Start at Westminster Bridge (Big Ben views), walk along the South Bank past the London Eye, Tate Modern, Shakespeare's Globe, to Tower Bridge. Takes 90 minutes and covers London's greatest hits.

Affordable Accommodation

Where to stay without breaking the bank:

  • Hostels in King's Cross/St Pancras — Central location, well-connected. Dorms from £25/night.
  • Budget hotels in Shoreditch — Trendy area with great food. Hub by Premier Inn offers tiny but clean rooms from £40.
  • Aparthotels in Zone 2 — Kitchenette means you save on food. Check Bermondsey and Elephant & Castle.

Free and Cheap Experiences

  • Watch the Changing of the Guard at Buckingham Palace (free, check schedule)
  • Explore Camden Market (free to browse, cheap street food)
  • Walk through Hyde Park, Regent's Park, or Hampstead Heath
  • Visit the Sky Garden — free rooftop garden with London panorama (book ahead)
  • Attend evensong at St Paul's Cathedral — free entry for the service, beautiful singing

Is London really doable on $100/day?

Yes, if you stay in hostels or budget hotels, eat from markets and supermarkets, and stick to free museums. You'll need to be intentional about spending, but you won't feel deprived.

What's the cheapest way to get from Heathrow to central London?

The Elizabeth Line (formerly TfL Rail) costs about £5.50 off-peak with contactless and takes 35-50 minutes. The Heathrow Express is faster (15 min) but costs £25+. Never take a taxi.

Should I get the London Pass?

Usually no for budget travelers. Most of London's best museums are free. The London Pass mainly covers paid attractions like the Tower of London and Westminster Abbey. Do the math for your specific plans.

When is the cheapest time to visit London?

January to March (excluding half-term week in February). Hotel prices drop 30-40%, flights are cheapest, and attractions are less crowded. Weather is cold and gray, but you'll be in museums anyway.
