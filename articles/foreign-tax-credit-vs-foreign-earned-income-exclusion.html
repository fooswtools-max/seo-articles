Foreign Tax Credit vs Foreign Earned Income Exclusion

Published: 2026-03-29 | Return My Tax | returnmytax.com

Understanding Your Options as a US Expat

US citizens and permanent residents living abroad must report worldwide income to the IRS. To prevent double taxation, the tax code provides two primary mechanisms: the Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) claimed on Form 1116 and the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) claimed on Form 2555.

Choosing between these options, or using them in combination where allowed, can result in dramatically different tax outcomes. The right choice depends on your income level, the tax rates in your country of residence, and your overall financial situation.

Foreign Tax Credit Explained

The Foreign Tax Credit provides a dollar-for-dollar credit against your US tax liability for income taxes paid to a foreign government. If you live in a high-tax country like Israel, where income tax rates often exceed US rates, the FTC can eliminate your US tax liability entirely and generate excess credits that can be carried forward for up to ten years.

The FTC is calculated separately for different categories of income: general category income, passive category income, and other categories. This limitation means high taxes paid on earned income cannot offset US tax on investment income, and vice versa.

One key advantage of the FTC is that it does not prevent you from contributing to US retirement accounts or claiming other tax benefits that the FEIE would restrict.

Foreign Earned Income Exclusion Explained

The FEIE allows qualifying taxpayers to exclude up to a specified amount of foreign earned income from US taxation. For tax year 2025, the exclusion amount is approximately $130,000. This amount is adjusted annually for inflation.

To qualify, you must meet either the Bona Fide Residence Test or the Physical Presence Test. The Bona Fide Residence Test requires establishing genuine residence in a foreign country. The Physical Presence Test requires being physically present in a foreign country for at least 330 full days during a 12-month period.

The FEIE also includes a housing exclusion that allows you to exclude or deduct certain housing expenses above a base amount. This can provide additional tax savings for expats in high-cost-of-living areas.

Which Should You Choose?

In high-tax countries like Israel, the Foreign Tax Credit is generally more beneficial because Israeli tax rates often exceed US rates at comparable income levels. The FTC allows you to apply excess credits to offset US tax on other income categories and carry forward unused credits.

The FEIE may be preferable if you live in a low-tax or no-tax country, have income below the exclusion threshold, or want to simplify your tax filing. It can also be advantageous in specific situations involving self-employment tax planning.

You can use the FEIE and FTC together, but you cannot claim the FTC for taxes paid on income that you have already excluded using the FEIE. Additionally, revoking a FEIE election prevents you from re-electing for five years without IRS approval, so consider your decision carefully.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use both the Foreign Tax Credit and FEIE?

Yes, you can use both in the same tax year, but not on the same income. You can exclude earned income using the FEIE and then claim the FTC for taxes paid on income not covered by the exclusion, such as investment income. You cannot claim FTC for taxes attributable to excluded income.

Which is better for Americans in Israel: FTC or FEIE?

For most Americans in Israel, the Foreign Tax Credit is more beneficial because Israeli income tax rates are generally higher than US rates. The FTC eliminates US tax liability and generates carry-forward credits. The FEIE limits retirement account contributions and may not fully offset US tax if your income exceeds the exclusion amount.

Can I switch between FTC and FEIE?

You can switch from FTC to FEIE in any year. However, if you revoke a FEIE election, you cannot re-elect FEIE for five tax years without IRS approval. This asymmetry means you should think carefully before switching away from the FEIE, as returning to it requires a waiting period or IRS consent.
