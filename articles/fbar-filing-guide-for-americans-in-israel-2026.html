FBAR Filing Guide for Americans in Israel 2026

Published: 2026-03-29 | Return My Tax | returnmytax.com

What Is FBAR and Who Must File?

The Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FBAR), officially FinCEN Form 114, must be filed by any US person who has a financial interest in or signature authority over foreign financial accounts with an aggregate value exceeding $10,000 at any point during the calendar year.

For Americans living in Israel, this includes Israeli bank accounts, investment accounts, pension funds (keren hishtalmut, kupat gemel), life insurance policies with cash value, and accounts at Israeli financial institutions. Joint accounts and accounts where you have signature authority but no financial interest must also be reported.

FBAR Filing Requirements and Deadlines

The FBAR must be filed electronically through the BSA E-Filing System by April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15. No separate extension request is needed. The filing covers the previous calendar year.

You must report the maximum value of each account during the year, converted to US dollars using the Treasury Department's end-of-year exchange rate. Even accounts that were opened and closed during the year must be reported if the aggregate balance threshold was met.

Israeli accounts that commonly trigger FBAR requirements include regular checking and savings accounts at banks like Bank Leumi, Bank Hapoalim, and Discount Bank, as well as keren hishtalmut funds, pension accounts, and investment portfolios.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

FBAR penalties are severe. Non-willful violations can result in penalties up to $10,000 per violation. Willful violations carry penalties of the greater of $100,000 or 50% of the account balance at the time of the violation, plus potential criminal prosecution.

If you have unfiled FBARs from previous years, do not ignore the situation. Several IRS programs exist to help delinquent filers come into compliance, including the Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures for qualifying taxpayers.

Step-by-Step Filing Instructions

Gather statements from all Israeli financial institutions showing maximum balances for the reporting year. Convert all balances to US dollars using the official Treasury exchange rate published on the IRS website.

Access the BSA E-Filing System at fincen.gov and create an account if you do not already have one. Complete FinCEN Form 114 online, entering details for each foreign account including the financial institution name, account number, account type, and maximum value.

After submission, save your confirmation number and a copy of the filed report. Keep your supporting documentation for at least six years from the filing date, as required by federal law.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to report my Israeli pension on FBAR?

Yes, Israeli pension funds including keren hishtalmut, kupat gemel, and bituach menahalim accounts must be reported on FBAR if your aggregate foreign account balances exceed $10,000 at any point during the year. Report the maximum value of each pension account during the reporting year.

What exchange rate should I use for FBAR?

Use the US Treasury's end-of-year exchange rate for converting foreign currency to US dollars on the FBAR. This rate is published on the IRS website and applies to all accounts regardless of when the maximum balance occurred during the year.

Can I file FBAR myself or do I need a professional?

You can file FBAR yourself through the BSA E-Filing System at no cost. However, if you have complex financial situations, multiple account types, or are filing delinquent FBARs, consulting a tax professional experienced with US expat taxation is recommended to ensure compliance and minimize penalties.
