How to Report Israeli Bank Accounts to IRS

Published: 2026-03-29 | Return My Tax | returnmytax.com

US Reporting Requirements for Israeli Accounts

Americans with Israeli bank accounts face multiple reporting requirements. The two primary forms are the FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) for accounts exceeding $10,000 in aggregate value and FATCA Form 8938 for specified foreign financial assets exceeding higher thresholds. These are separate requirements with different rules, thresholds, and filing methods.

Failure to comply with these requirements can result in substantial penalties. Understanding which forms you need to file, what accounts to report, and how to determine account values is essential for every American with Israeli financial ties.

FBAR Requirements for Israeli Accounts

If the combined value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any time during the calendar year, you must file an FBAR. This includes Israeli checking accounts, savings accounts, investment accounts, keren hishtalmut, pension funds, and any account at a non-US financial institution where you have signature authority.

File the FBAR electronically through the BSA E-Filing System by April 15 (automatic extension to October 15). Report the maximum value of each account during the year in US dollars using the Treasury's year-end exchange rate.

Even accounts with small balances count toward the $10,000 threshold. If you have three Israeli accounts with maximum balances of $4,000 each, totaling $12,000, all three accounts must be reported on the FBAR.

FATCA Form 8938 Requirements

Form 8938 is filed with your tax return and has higher thresholds than FBAR. For US persons living abroad filing jointly, reporting is required when foreign assets exceed $400,000 at year-end or $600,000 at any point during the year. For single filers abroad, the thresholds are $200,000 and $300,000 respectively.

Form 8938 covers a broader range of assets than FBAR, including foreign stock or securities not held in a financial account, foreign partnership interests, and foreign financial instruments issued by non-US entities. However, it specifically excludes assets held in US-based accounts.

Unlike FBAR, Form 8938 is filed as an attachment to your annual tax return. If you receive a filing extension for your tax return, the Form 8938 deadline extends as well.

Common Israeli Accounts and How to Report Them

Israeli bank accounts at institutions like Bank Leumi, Bank Hapoalim, Mizrahi Tefahot, and Discount Bank are reported with the bank's name, address, account number, and maximum value. For multi-currency accounts, report each currency sub-account separately if they have distinct account numbers.

Keren hishtalmut (advanced study funds) and pension accounts require special attention. These are considered foreign financial accounts for FBAR purposes. Obtain annual statements from your Israeli financial institutions showing account values in shekels, then convert to dollars.

Brokerage accounts at Israeli firms and investment house accounts must also be reported. Include the total value of all securities and cash held in these accounts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to file both FBAR and Form 8938?

Possibly. FBAR and Form 8938 are separate requirements with different thresholds. You may need to file both, one, or neither depending on your account values. Filing one does not satisfy the other. Many Americans in Israel with significant financial assets must file both forms.

What happens if I forgot to report Israeli accounts in past years?

If you have unreported foreign accounts, you can use the IRS Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures to come into compliance without facing full penalties. For taxpayers residing abroad, the streamlined program typically waives penalties. The longer you wait to correct the situation, the greater the potential risk.

Are Israeli joint accounts reported differently?

For FBAR purposes, the full value of a joint account is attributed to each joint owner. Both you and your spouse must report the full maximum value on your respective FBARs if filing separately. For Form 8938 on a joint return, report joint accounts once with an indication that the account is jointly owned.
