Step-by-Step: How to File Small Claims in Florida

Step 1: Send a Demand Letter. Before filing, send a formal demand letter to the defendant. This resolves approximately 30% of disputes without going to court. A demand letter shows the judge you tried to resolve the matter first. Generate a free demand letter here.
Step 2: Gather Your Evidence. Collect all relevant documentation: contracts, receipts, text messages, emails, photographs, invoices, and any written agreements. Timestamped photos from your phone are valid evidence in Florida small claims court.
Step 3: File Your Claim. Visit your local County Court or file online (where available). You will need the defendant's full legal name and address. Pay the filing fee ($55-$300), which you can add to your claim amount if you win.
Step 4: Serve the Defendant. Florida requires the defendant to be formally notified. This can be done by certified mail, a process server, or the sheriff's office. Keep proof of service.
Step 5: Attend Your Hearing. Arrive early, dress professionally, and bring organized copies of all evidence. The judge will hear both sides. Most hearings last 15-30 minutes. Present your case clearly and calmly with facts and documentation.
Step 6: Collect Your Judgment. If you win, the defendant is ordered to pay. If they refuse, Florida allows wage garnishment, bank levies, and property liens to enforce the judgment.

Common Cases in Florida Small Claims Court

  • Security deposit disputes — Landlord kept your deposit without proper itemized deductions
  • Contractor disputes — Work not completed, poor quality, or contractor disappeared
  • Property damage — Neighbor, roommate, or other party damaged your property
  • Unpaid debts — Someone owes you money and refuses to pay
  • Auto repair disputes — Mechanic overcharged or performed unnecessary work
  • Breach of contract — The other party failed to honor a written or verbal agreement

Tips for Winning Your Case in Florida

  1. Document everything — Photos, screenshots, emails, and texts are powerful evidence
  2. Be organized — Present your evidence in chronological order with clear labels
  3. Stay calm — Judges respond to facts, not emotions. Let your evidence speak.
  4. Know your numbers — Calculate your exact damages with receipts and invoices
  5. Send a demand letter first — It shows good faith and resolves 30% of cases without court
