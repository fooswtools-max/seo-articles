Expat Tax Filing Step-by-Step Guide 2026

Updated April 2026 · ReturnMyTax

Expat Tax Filing Step-by-Step

Everything US citizens and green card holders abroad need to know for 2026 filing

The Key Rule: US Citizens Must File Worldwide

The United States is one of only two countries (the other is Eritrea) that taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. If you are a US citizen or green card holder, you must file a US tax return even if you live and work abroad and owe zero US tax.

Step 1: Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)

1 Form 2555: Exclude up to $126,500 (2026) of foreign earned income from US tax.

To qualify, you must pass ONE of these tests:

  • Bona Fide Residence Test: You are a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an entire tax year
  • Physical Presence Test: You are physically present in a foreign country for 330 full days in any 12-month period
Tip: The Physical Presence Test is easier to meet and does not require a specific visa type. Track your travel days carefully — even a single day over the 35-day US limit can disqualify you.

Step 2: Foreign Housing Exclusion

In addition to the FEIE, you can exclude qualifying housing costs above a base amount (approximately $18,976 in 2026). This covers:

  • Rent or fair rental value of employer-provided housing
  • Utilities (except telephone)
  • Property insurance
  • Parking (residential)

Step 3: FBAR Filing (FinCEN 114)

If the combined value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file an FBAR.

  • Filed electronically at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov
  • Includes bank accounts, investment accounts, pension accounts, life insurance with cash value
  • Penalties for non-filing: Up to $12,909 per account per year (non-willful); up to $129,210 or 50% of account balance (willful)

Step 4: FATCA Reporting (Form 8938)

If your foreign financial assets exceed these thresholds, file Form 8938 with your tax return:

Filing StatusLiving Abroad Threshold
Single / Married Filing Separately$200,000 end of year OR $300,000 at any time
Married Filing Jointly$400,000 end of year OR $600,000 at any time

Step 5: Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116)

If you pay income tax to a foreign country, you can claim a credit on your US return. This is sometimes better than the FEIE — especially if you earn above $126,500 or your foreign tax rate is higher than US rates.

FEIE vs. Foreign Tax Credit: You generally cannot use both on the same income. If your foreign tax rate is higher than your US rate, the Foreign Tax Credit is usually better. If your foreign tax rate is lower, the FEIE saves more.

Expat Filing Checklist

  • Gather all income documents (US and foreign)
  • Track days present in each country for Physical Presence Test
  • List all foreign bank/investment accounts for FBAR
  • Calculate total foreign financial assets for FATCA (Form 8938)
  • Decide between FEIE (Form 2555) and Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116)
  • File FBAR electronically at FinCEN by April 15 (auto-extended to Oct 15)
  • File Form 1040 with Forms 2555 and/or 1116 attached
  • Check tax treaty benefits between US and your country of residence
